## General Ideas (5)

**Idea 1: MediMeal**
Most people on prescription medications have no idea their drugs interact with everyday foods — grapefruit with statins, dairy with antibiotics, vitamin K with blood thinners, alcohol with antihistamines. MediMeal lets you log your medications and flags food interactions before meals, suggesting optimal timing ("take this 2 hours before eating") and a list of foods to avoid. Fills the gap that pharmacists mention once and patients forget immediately.

**Idea 2: GiftLog**
Buying gifts for the same people every year is a recurring blank — you can't remember what you already gave them in 2023, and you've lost the mental note about the book they mentioned wanting in October. GiftLog is a simple gift-giving journal: log gifts given per person with date and occasion, capture "wishlist hints" they drop throughout the year, and surface their history next time a birthday approaches. The private, low-maintenance version of something everyone wishes existed.

**Idea 3: PhotoCull**
After every trip or event, people have 300 near-identical photos but curate exactly zero of them — the dump sits in camera roll forever, unshared, unseen. PhotoCull analyzes a batch of photos, removes duplicates and blurry shots, picks the single best frame of each moment, and creates a tidy 20–30 shot album ready to share or print. Solves the paralysis between "take the photos" and "actually do something with them."

**Idea 4: PaperPile**
Everyone has a physical pile of paper they can't throw away — appliance manuals, council notices, vehicle inspection reminders, tax correspondence, lease agreements. Photograph each document, and PaperPile categorizes it automatically, extracts key dates (expiry, deadline, renewal), and reminds you before they matter. Turns the anxiety pile into a manageable archive with zero manual filing.

**Idea 5: MatchMeal**
When a household has multiple dietary restrictions — one person is celiac, another is diabetic, a kid refuses vegetables — finding meals everyone can eat together requires manual mental Tetris every single night. MatchMeal is a shared meal planner where each household member has a restriction profile; it only suggests and plans meals that work for everyone at the table. Not a generic recipe app — specifically the "what can we all actually eat" filtering layer that no recipe app gets right.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: ErrorBrief 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Developers get raw crash alerts from Sentry or Bugsnag — noisy, technical, often ignored — but have no easy way to understand which errors are actually hurting users today versus background noise. ErrorBrief connects to your error monitoring tool and sends a daily plain-English digest: "12 users hit a checkout failure this afternoon, likely a race condition on payment confirmation — here are the 3 affected user IDs and the stack trace." Turns alert fatigue into actionable daily triage without a dedicated ops person.

**Idea 7: BetaBoard 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Getting quality early testers for a new app or SaaS is one of the hardest early-stage problems — posting to subreddits gets lurkers, Product Hunt beta lists are passive, and friends give polite non-feedback. BetaBoard is a curated marketplace where indie founders post beta slots with their target user persona, and testers are screened for past feedback quality (tracked via ratings from previous betas they joined). Matches real users who actually try things and write structured feedback — not just email signups that never log in.

**Idea 8: RefactoRisk 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
When developers plan a refactor or want to pay down technical debt, they have no objective way to prioritize which parts of the codebase are safe to touch first versus which are landmines. RefactoRisk analyzes your git history and test coverage to build a visual "risk heatmap" of the codebase — files ranked by change frequency × low test coverage = highest blast radius if you break them. Turns gut-feel refactoring sequencing into a data-driven prioritization list that engineering teams can actually plan sprints around.
