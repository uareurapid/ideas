## General Ideas (5)

**Idea 1: TenantVet**
Small landlords with 1-3 properties can't justify €100+ per professional background check but have no way to verify tenant applications beyond gut feel. A lightweight tenant verification service that cross-references name + address against public records (eviction filings, outstanding court judgments, bankruptcy) and returns a simple risk score for €15/check — no property management software subscription required. Targets the largest rental market segment (mom-and-pop landlords) that the big screening tools ignore because they're designed for agencies managing 500+ units.

**Idea 2: HandMeDown**
Parents of multiple children lose track of where outgrown clothes, shoes, toys, and sports equipment went — was the winter coat passed to the neighbor's kid, donated, or waiting in the attic? HandMeDown is a private family hand-me-down tracker: log items as they move between families or bins, tag by size and season, set "call it back" reminders for items you might need for a younger child, and filter what's currently available for each age bracket. The logistics layer that every extended family with kids currently operates on voice notes and shoeboxes under the bed.

**Idea 3: VocalRest**
Teachers, podcasters, call center agents, vocal coaches, and anyone who speaks for a living has no awareness of daily voice strain until they wake up voiceless. VocalRest runs a lightweight periodic microphone check during your workday, estimates cumulative vocal load (decibel-minutes × frequency range), and warns you before damage sets in — "you've been talking for 3 hours today — schedule 30 minutes of silence." Like a step counter for your vocal cords, backed by the same phonotrauma research that speech therapists use.

**Idea 4: MiniStack**
Miniature painting and model building (Warhammer, Gunpla, Lego, scale dioramas) is one of the fastest-growing hobby categories on TikTok and YouTube, yet the community has zero dedicated software to manage anything. MiniStack is a build-in-progress log and paint inventory: photograph your paints to auto-ID by brand/color, track builds across days/weeks with photo progression, log which paints and techniques you used per model, and generate a shopping list for your next project. No more "I know I own Citadel Mephiston Red but which drawer is it in?"

**Idea 5: LineNow**
Nobody wants to queue for a popular bakery, food truck, or weekend market only to discover the line snakes around the block — but most places don't publish real-time wait info. LineNow is a zero-friction crowd-sourced queue status layer: one tap to report the current line at any location (empty / short / medium / long / insane) with a "reported X minutes ago" freshness timestamp. No account, no check-in, no social feed — just the civic service of knowing if the croissant is worth the wait before you leave the house.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: BundleSlicer 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Mobile developers chasing sub-100MB app binary sizes have only blunt tools — Xcode's size report and APK Analyzer show file-level data but not the per-feature cost of shipping a library or asset. BundleSlicer integrates into your CI and produces a feature-level cost report: "The image filters module costs 3.2MB (1.8MB library + 1.4MB assets) and is used by 8% of users." Enables data-driven decisions about what to lazy-load, replace, or drop entirely — without guessing which obscure dependency is actually bloating your build.

**Idea 7: EnvVault 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Every team shares .env files through Slack, Notion, or encrypted ZIPs — creating a security and onboarding nightmare where someone inevitably commits a real API key or a new hire spends 3 hours hunting down which env vars go where. EnvVault is a lightweight, local-first .env manager: sync approved files with role-based access, rotate secrets with one click, auto-generate .env.example with documentation from your schema. Like 1Password for environment variables, designed specifically for the "first day on a new project" setup ritual that currently eats every developer's morning.

**Idea 8: PromptDiff 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Teams using AI coding tools (Claude, Cursor, Copilot, ChatGPT) generate code whose intent and reasoning is invisible to reviewers — there's no record of what prompt produced what output, making code review and future refactoring a guessing game. PromptDiff is a CLI + IDE extension that captures each AI interaction during development, attaches the prompt context to generated code as a lightweight metadata annotation, and surfaces prompt/code pairs during code review. The git blame for AI-suggested changes — finally answers "why did the AI write it this way?"

## Mobile App Ideas (2)

**Idea 9: PlateProof 📱 MOBILE APP**
Every used car buyer on Facebook Marketplace or OLX wonders if the seller is legit — odometer rollbacks, stolen vehicles, and outstanding finance are rampant in private sales, and running a full CarVertical report costs €25-40 per car. PlateProof lets you snap one photo (license plate + VIN sticker on the door jamb) and instantly cross-references both against public databases for red flags — reported stolen, mileage discrepancy across MOT/test history discrepancies, outstanding finance — returning a plain-English "buy with confidence / investigate further / run away" verdict in 30 seconds. **Low competition:** commercial vehicle history tools are slow and expensive per check; a lightweight app for the "snap and know" quick check at the viewing doesn't exist. **Viral angle:** "Snapped a plate on a car that looked pristine — PlateProof found it was on finance with €6k outstanding and had a secret odometer rollback. Saved me from a nightmare" is the exact story that spreads through car groups and marketplace communities like wildfire.

**Idea 10: MultiTrack 📱 MOBILE APP**
The phone's built-in timer app caps at one active timer — useless when you're multitasking in the kitchen, workshop, or anywhere else you need to track 5+ overlapping countdowns simultaneously. MultiTrack is a voice-commanded, glanceable multi-timer designed for cooks who need "steak 3 minutes, roasted potatoes 20 minutes, resting 5 minutes, don't forget the garlic bread at 4 minutes" — all running at once on a single screen with voice input ("start steak for 3 minutes"), tap-to-extend, and a widget for at-a-glance status. No recipe databases, no bloated cooking app — just faster, smarter timers for people who actually cook. **Low competition:** the App Store has timer apps with recipe databases, calendar timer apps, and "focus timers" — none are a dead-simple multi-track voice timer for the kitchen. **Funny/viral angle:** "MultiTrack handling Christmas dinner: 14 active timers, turkey is winning, gravy is about to lose, and I'm the hero of the kitchen with my phone in widget mode" is the exact relatable chaos content that gets shared in cooking groups and family WhatsApp chats.
