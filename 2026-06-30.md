## General Ideas (5)

**Idea 1: FoodCost**
Restaurant "shrinkflation" is everywhere — portions shrink while prices climb — but consumers can only sense it, not prove it. FoodCost lets diners log meals with photos and portion estimates, then tracks price-per-gram over time across repeat visits and similar restaurants, building a crowd-sourced shrinkflation index by city and chain. B2C angle is the data-nerd + outrage appeal; B2B angle is selling competitive menu pricing intelligence to restaurants adjusting their own portions and pricing strategies.

**Idea 2: TableTonight**
Walk-in diners at popular restaurants waste evenings waiting 45–90 minutes with zero visibility into when they'll be seated. TableTonight is Waze for restaurant wait times — diners already waiting report their arrival time and party size, creating a real-time wait map that predicts table turnover from historical dining durations. Restaurants can claim their listing and post accurate estimates, turning the wait-time guessing game into a predictable experience.

**Idea 3: ChecklistKit**
Small businesses — restaurants, salons, dental offices, auto shops — are required to maintain opening/closing checklists, cleaning logs, and health inspection readiness, but they still use paper clipboards that nobody actually verifies. ChecklistKit is a simplified digital checklist app with photo verification ("snap the clean fry station"), manager override for skipped items, auto-generated inspection readiness reports, and a mystery-shopper mode that tests staff compliance. For the millions of small businesses where paper checklists get faked.

**Idea 4: WardrobeMirror**
Selling clothes on Poshmark, Depop, or Vinted means photographing, measuring, and writing descriptions for each piece — a massive friction that causes people to donate instead of resell. WardrobeMirror uses your phone camera to scan a hung garment, auto-detects the brand, size, and measurements via AI, generates a flattering listing description, and cross-posts to all platforms in one tap. For the 60M+ secondhand clothing sellers who hate the listing grind.

**Idea 5: FreezeSaver**
Households waste $1,500/year on food that spoils before it's eaten — not because people forget it exists, but because they don't know *which* items are about to turn and what can be salvaged. FreezeSaver identifies fridge items approaching spoilage (from barcode-scanned purchase dates + shelf-life data), flags what can be frozen instead of tossed, and maintains a freezer inventory with expiration dates. The "freeze or lose it" decision engine that prevents food waste at the critical moment.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: FlowDeadEnd 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Analytics tell you *where* users churn in onboarding, but never *why* — was it confusion, a dead-end bug, or just low motivation? FlowDeadEnd records short opt-in selfie reaction videos from beta testers during onboarding flows, time-synced with screen recording, so you can see exactly what made them frown, pause, or say "wait, what?" Combines the qualitative depth of user interviews with the scale of product analytics — no scheduling calls, no $500/user research sessions. For product teams that can't afford professional UX research but need to understand drop-off.

**Idea 7: ConfigCrash 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Roughly half of production outages are caused by config changes, not code bugs — a misconfigured env var, a bad feature flag value, a rotated API key with a typo. ConfigCrash hooks into your deployment pipeline and validates every config change against a defined schema with pre-deployment dry runs showing exactly what will change and whether anything will break. Think TypeScript for configuration — catches `TIMEOUT="true"` before it takes down production at 3 AM. For teams that learned the hard way that YAML doesn't do type checking.

**Idea 8: ReverseJobBoard 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Developer job boards are flooded with the same "full-stack ninja rockstar, 6 years of React" copy-paste postings. ReverseJobBoard flips the dynamic — developers post "here's what I'm looking for" profiles (tech stack, salary range, remote preference, ideal team size) and companies apply to them directly. Like Hired but indie/bootstrapped-company friendly, no recruiter gatekeeping, transparent salary bands upfront. For senior devs tired of filtering through 500 identical listings to find 3 real opportunities.

## Mobile App Ideas (2)

**Idea 9: HamNet 📱 MOBILE APP**
Amateur radio operators (3M+ worldwide, with growing interest from prepper and off-grid communities) coordinate entirely through 1990s-era websites and printed frequency charts — their hobby software is frozen in time. HamNet shows real-time ionospheric propagation conditions, active repeaters near your GPS location, scheduled nets and contest calendars, and a mobile contact-logging system (QSOs) that auto-uploads to Logbook of the World. Niche: ham radio community — passionate, aging but expanding, and fully willing to pay for specialized tools. Low competition because existing ham apps look like Windows 95 and haven't been updated in a decade. The ARRL alone has 150K+ members searching for modern mobile tools.

**Idea 10: ExText 📱 MOBILE APP**
Send a risky text — but only if your designated friend approves it first. Before you fire off that "we need to talk," the angry reply, or the 2 AM confession, ExText holds it in a quarantine queue where your "text editor" friend reviews, edits, vetoes, or greenlights it. Think "don't text your ex" meets Grammarly tone check meets best-friend intervention — absurd but genuinely useful. Niche: anyone who's ever sent a text they regretted (everyone), with built-in virality from sharing screenshots of the review queue. Low competition because it's too silly for VC-backed startups but solves a real, universal, deeply relatable problem. The App Store has a dozen drunk-dial blockers but zero "my friend has to approve this before it ruins my social life" apps.
