## General Ideas (5)

**Idea 1: FuneralBudget**
Families arranging a funeral are at their most emotionally vulnerable, pressured by time, and systematically upsold by funeral homes on everything from coffins to flower arrangements — yet nobody ever explains that the "basic" legal requirements cost a fraction of what's presented as standard. FuneralBudget is a plain-language guide + cost estimator for funeral planning: understand what's legally required vs. optional, compare package prices against itemised alternatives, and know what to say (and refuse) when walking into a funeral director's office. The price transparency layer for the one industry that exploits grief.

**Idea 2: SchoolBagAudit**
Primary school children carry wildly different bags every day — PE kit on Tuesdays, recorder on Thursdays, permission slip due Friday — and parents rely on a fragmented mix of school newsletter, WhatsApp groups, and the child's ability to relay information. SchoolBagAudit is a recurring checklist per child, keyed to their specific timetable: get a Sunday evening reminder showing exactly what goes in the bag tomorrow and why, with term-specific events (swimming, school trip, photo day) pulled from a shared class calendar. Less "oh no, it's recorder day and we forgot again," more just the right thing in the bag every morning.

**Idea 3: MechanicTranslator**
When a mechanic calls with "your car needs a new timing chain tensioner, gasket set, and an oil pressure sensor — that's €680," most people have no idea whether that's reasonable, urgent, or an upsell. MechanicTranslator lets you paste or voice-input the repair list, and returns: plain-English explanation of what each part does, typical cost range for parts + labour, whether each repair is genuinely urgent or safely deferrable, and the right follow-up questions to ask. Not a replacement for a trusted mechanic — just enough knowledge to not feel completely powerless on the phone.

**Idea 4: WeddingVendorReview**
Wedding vendors — photographers, caterers, florists, planners — are almost impossible to evaluate fairly because (1) reviews skew heavily positive due to the emotional context, (2) bad experiences rarely get published because couples don't want their wedding memories associated with a complaint, and (3) the review window is narrow (people post right after, not 6 months in when they discover the photos were disappointing). WeddingVendorReview is a delayed + verified review platform: couples submit a full review 6 months post-wedding, reviews are verified against booking confirmation, and the platform surfaces "what went wrong" breakdowns alongside the overall score. Honest signal for the highest-spend day of most people's lives.

**Idea 5: FrequencyFinder**
Local radio, community events, and neighbourhood announcements still happen on frequencies (FM, DAB, bulletin boards, local Facebook groups, WhatsApp neighbourhood groups) that newcomers to an area simply don't know exist — you have to already know someone to know where to look. FrequencyFinder is a searchable directory of hyper-local information channels by postcode/area: the local Whatsapp group for your street, the Facebook group for your market town, the community radio station, the neighbourhood Facebook page, the Nextdoor alternative. Onboarding for anyone who's just moved — the "where is everyone actually talking?" answer in one search.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: AbTestSignal 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Solo founders and small teams know they should A/B test their landing page, pricing page, and onboarding flow — but setting up Optimizely or even a homegrown variant system takes 2–3 days of engineering work that never makes the priority list, so decisions default to whoever shouts loudest in the founder Slack. AbTestSignal is a zero-code A/B testing layer for indie SaaS: paste a script tag, define variants in a simple dashboard (different headline, CTA, pricing layout), set your success metric (signup, trial start, upgrade), and get statistical significance alerts when a winner emerges — without a single line of your codebase touched. The A/B test layer founders keep deprioritising, finally small enough to install in 15 minutes.

**Idea 7: DocOutdater 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
The biggest waste of developer time that nobody tracks is finding outdated documentation — a Stack Overflow answer from 2019 that uses a deprecated API, a library README that still shows the v1 setup, or an internal wiki page that describes an architecture you migrated away from 18 months ago. DocOutdater is a documentation staleness detector: connect it to your GitHub repos or paste a URL, and it flags code examples using deprecated patterns (cross-referenced against official changelogs), links pointing to outdated API docs, and internal pages whose referenced code no longer matches what's in the repo. Run it once before a new hire starts, or weekly as a documentation health check.

**Idea 8: LaunchEmailKit 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Indie developers know they should build an email sequence for new trial signups — welcome, day 3 tip, day 7 "did you try X," day 14 conversion push — but writing those 6–8 emails that sound human and not like a marketing robot takes a full weekend they never have. LaunchEmailKit generates a complete onboarding email sequence from your product's feature list and target audience: paste your landing page URL, answer 5 questions about what your app does and who it's for, and receive a 7-email sequence with subject lines, body copy, and timing logic — editable, ready to drop into any ESP. Not just AI slop; a sensible sequence structure with real copywriting frameworks baked in.

## Mobile App Ideas (2)

**Idea 9: SeedSwap 📱 MOBILE APP**
Gardeners and allotment holders accumulate far more seeds than they can grow — packets bought on impulse, saved seeds from last year's harvest, half-used envelopes — but have no way to connect with other local growers to swap them, so perfectly viable seeds go in a drawer and die. SeedSwap is a hyperlocal seed exchange app: list what you have (with surplus quantity and age), browse what's available within 5 miles, request a swap by post or meetup, and track your seed inventory through the season. **Low competition:** the gardening app market is dominated by watering reminders and plant ID — a physical exchange/barter mechanic for seeds is completely untouched. **Funny/viral angle:** "I traded 40 tomato seeds for someone's entire chilli haul and have zero regrets" is exact allotment Twitter energy, and the inherent wholesomeness of strangers exchanging seeds through the post will absolutely go viral in the right communities.

**Idea 10: QueueRager 📱 MOBILE APP**
Nobody has ever built a tool specifically for the universal low-grade fury of waiting in line — theme parks, post offices, government offices, phone holds — that both helps you estimate actual wait time using crowdsourced data AND lets you vent constructively into a community of people suffering alongside you at this exact moment. QueueRager is equal parts wait-time tracker and solidarity app: check in to any queue, see estimates from other people currently waiting, log your actual wait for future users, and read/post one-sentence "current vibes" from fellow queue sufferers ("been here 40 min, the man in front has literally 11 parcels"). **Low competition:** queue time apps exist for Disneyland; nobody has made the general-purpose crowdsourced wait-time + communal suffering layer. **Funny/viral angle:** the "one-sentence queue dispatches" feature alone will generate screenshot content — "Post Office queue, 11:15am Monday: 'The printer has broken and we are all going to die here'" is the exact tweet that goes everywhere.
