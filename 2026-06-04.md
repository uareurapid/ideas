## General Ideas (5)

**Idea 1: MileageHero**
Self-employed people, freelancers, and tradies lose hundreds in tax deductions every year because they can't reconstruct business trip mileage from memory at year-end. MileageHero auto-detects trips over a configurable distance, lets you tag each one as business/personal with one swipe, and exports a fully-compliant HMRC/IRS mileage log at tax time with totals, routes, and purposes. The difference between a forgotten habit and £800 back in your pocket.

**Idea 2: CalibrationLog**
People who own precision equipment — espresso machines, 3D printers, lab scales, torque wrenches, photographic lenses, audiophile gear — have no dedicated place to record when they last calibrated something, by how much it drifted, and what adjustment was made. CalibrationLog is a maintenance diary for precision equipment: log each calibration event with drift measurement and notes, set reminder intervals per device, and export a calibration history for warranty claims or resale. Niche but deeply valued by the precision-obsessed community that currently uses spreadsheets.

**Idea 3: StageDirector**
Amateur dramatics groups manage scripts, rehearsal schedules, costumes, props, and cast logistics across a chaos of WhatsApp messages, shared Google Docs, and a notebook someone left at the venue. StageDirector is a production management micro-tool for amateur theatre: cast list with roles, scene-by-scene blocking notes, costume and props tracker per actor, rehearsal attendance log, and a countdown to opening night. Not professional theatre software — just the coordinator's tool the am-dram world doesn't have and desperately needs every panto season.

**Idea 4: ChildHandbook**
When grandparents, new babysitters, or a friend watches your kid for the first time, parents do a verbal handover that everyone immediately forgets — resulting in a wrong nap time, a missed allergy, or an anxious call to ask where the plasters are. ChildHandbook is a shareable care briefing app: create a child's profile with daily routine, allergies, medications, emergency contacts, and bedtime rules, then share a link with any caregiver before they arrive. Not a monitoring app — just a "how to look after my kid today" card that stays current because it takes 30 seconds to update.

**Idea 5: MoodRooms**
Couples and housemates working from home have no lightweight way to signal their availability status to the people physically in the same building — leading to interrupted deep work sessions, awkward door-knocking, and passive-aggressive Post-its. Like Slack statuses, but for the people you actually live with.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: DirectoryBlast 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Indie developers know they should submit their product to 50+ launch directories — Product Hunt, BetaList, Hacker News, SaaS directories, niche communities — but the process is so tedious (each site has a different form, different requirements, different account) that most founders submit to three and give up. DirectoryBlast maintains a curated database of 150+ submission targets, lets you fill in your product details once, auto-fills forms where possible, and tracks submission status and backlink value per directory. Turn a three-day chore into a three-hour launch sprint — and finally get the SEO backlinks you keep not building.

**Idea 7: OnCallHandbook 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
When a developer joins an on-call rotation, they inherit a runbook that's 60% outdated, missing the three things that actually go wrong at 2am, and hasn't been touched since the engineer who wrote it left the company. OnCallHandbook is a living incident knowledge base that self-updates: when an alert fires and an engineer resolves it, a frictionless post-resolution prompt captures "what happened + how I fixed it" in two sentences, tagged to the alert type. Over 3 months it auto-curates a "what actually breaks and how to fix it" playbook that stays current precisely because the cost of updating it is near-zero — no Confluence page, no ticket, just a prompt while the memory is fresh.

**Idea 8: SDKHealthCheck 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Mobile app developers relying on 10–20 third-party SDKs have no equivalent of `npm audit` for their CocoaPods, Gradle, or Swift Package Manager dependencies — they find out a SDK is three major versions behind (or has a critical crash bug) when users start emailing. SDKHealthCheck connects to your iOS or Android repo, scans your dependency files weekly, and delivers a report showing: outdated SDK versions, known CVEs, binary size impact per SDK, and deprecation warnings. "Your RevenueCat SDK is 4 versions behind and there's a confirmed in-app purchase crash in your version" before your next App Store review, not after.

## Mobile App Ideas (2)

**Idea 9: DiceHoard 📱 MOBILE APP**
Tabletop RPG players and board game collectors accumulate sprawling dice collections — sometimes hundreds of individual dice across dozens of sets — but have no way to catalogue them, know what they own when shopping, or track the legendary "this d20 always rolls 1s" superstition with actual data. DiceHoard lets you photograph and log your dice sets, tag them to specific games or campaigns, and record session results to build a statistical "performance history" per die — so you can prove (or disprove) whether your lucky d20 is statistically cursed. **Low competition:** the TTRPG community is massive, deeply passionate about their dice, and has zero purpose-built collection apps. **Viral angle:** "My d20 has rolled a 1 on 26% of all critical moments across 18 months of data — it is scientifically cursed" is an exact viral screenshot in every D&D Discord server on the planet.

**Idea 10: NoiseBuddy 📱 MOBILE APP**
City apartment dwellers dealing with noisy neighbors, street noise, or thin walls have no tool to document whether the noise is objectively getting worse, what time it peaks, or whether their landlord's claim that "it's within normal limits" holds any water. NoiseBuddy runs passive background noise monitoring, logs decibel averages by hour of day over weeks and months, and lets you annotate spikes ("upstairs party," "garbage truck," "construction started") to build a noise diary you can export as a formatted report for tenant disputes, council complaints, or lease renewal decisions. **Low competition:** decibel meter apps exist for one-off readings; nobody has built the longitudinal noise diary with dispute-ready export. **Viral/funny angle:** "I have 8 months of acoustic evidence proving my landlord is lying" is a deeply satisfying story — and the kind of thing that gets screenshotted and posted in every renter's rights community instantly.
