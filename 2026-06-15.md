**Idea 1: DetourPlanner**

DetourPlanner finds "within 10 minutes of your route" hidden spots ranked by detour time vs. worth-it factor, curated by locals rather than algorithms. The "I've driven past that sign 50 times and never stopped" problem, solved with actual recommendations from people who live there.

**Idea 2: CommuteSong**
The morning commute sets the emotional tone for the entire day, but most people shuffle their library and end up with music that clashes with their mood or the weather — arriving either too hyped or in the wrong headspace. CommuteSong builds a playlist based on today's specific context: weather, upcoming meetings (stressful 9am or casual Friday), current energy level, and commute duration — generating a soundtrack that lands you at your destination in the right mental state. Not a music app, a mood-prep tool that happens to live in your Spotify queue.

**Idea 3: FurnitureFit**
Furniture bought online arrives looking dramatically different in your actual living room — wrong scale, wrong proportion, won't fit through the door — leading to millions of returns and a general sense of "it looked smaller in the photo." FurnitureFit uses your phone's LiDAR (or simple room measurement by walking the perimeter) to map your space, then compares against listed furniture dimensions with visual overlays, warning you if that sofa is 4cm too wide for the doorway or will crowd the coffee table. The "but will it actually fit in my actual flat?" answer before you click buy.

**Idea 4: TravelSOS**

The travel safety net that exists in your pocket but you only open when you actually need it.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: ErrorScreenshot 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
When a user reports a bug, indie developers get either "it crashed" or an angry paragraph with zero technical context — no console logs, no device info, no idea what the user was doing — turning every bug report into a 10-message back-and-forth. ErrorScreenshot embeds a single script tag that, on runtime error or manual "report a bug" trigger, captures the user's screen state, console output, last 5 user interactions, browser/device fingerprint, and network request status — all bundled into one clickable report with a screenshot. Like Sentry's breadcrumbs, but any error report comes with the visual context of what the user saw when it broke — cutting "need more info" cycles from hours to zero.

**Idea 7: PricingPageInspector 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Indie SaaS founders obsess over signup totals but have no idea what's happening in the 30 seconds between someone landing on their pricing page and either clicking "Start Free Trial" or bouncing — where they hovered, which tier they considered, what made them leave, and whether the annual vs. monthly toggle caused confusion. PricingPageInspector is a lightweight heatmap + session recorder designed specifically for pricing pages: it tracks tier hover dwell time, scroll depth across feature comparisons, abandonment points, and clicks on the "compare plans" toggle — then delivers a weekly "your pricing page is losing people at X point" analysis with suggested changes. Every other analytics tool gives you total pageviews; this one tells you why the person who viewed your pricing page didn't become a user.

**Idea 8: CodebaseGlossary 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Every codebase accumulates domain-specific jargon, internal abbreviations, and team-specific shorthand that becomes impenetrable to new hires — "widget batch," "pipeline flush," "customer-387 processing" — and the knowledge lives in the brains of the three people who've been there longest, creating a dependency that slows onboarding and creates bus-factor risk. CodebaseGlossary scans your repo (comments, variable names, PR descriptions, README) plus connected Slack channels, extracts domain-specific terms and abbreviations with their frequency and context, and builds a living, searchable glossary that new hires can browse or search before interrupting a senior engineer. The difference between "what does 'redgreen deploy' mean?" being a question for Slack vs. something you can look up in 3 seconds.

## Mobile App Ideas (2)

**Idea 9: DeskSpotter 📱 MOBILE APP**
Remote workers who rotate between coffee shops, libraries, and coworking spaces know that not all seats are equal — some have no outlets, WiFi drops in the back corner, the chair by the window is freezing in winter — but every app treats the venue as one rating when the seat-level experience varies wildly. DeskSpotter is a seat-by-seat review map for remote work spots: open the app at any cafe/co-work space, see a floor plan with pins on individual seats rated by outlet availability, WiFi speed (from actual speed tests), noise level, and comfort. Log your favorite seat and build a personal "desk map" of your neighborhood. **Low competition:** coworking discovery apps (like Croissant or Deskpass) list venues, not seats — seat-level spatial reviews with speed test data don't exist anywhere in the app store. **Funny/viral angle:** "The red armchair at the back of Grind Coffee has an outlet that only works if you plug your charger in at exactly 37 degrees — 42 people have confirmed this" is the exact kind of obsessively specific information remote workers screenshot and share in their WhatsApp groups like sacred scripture.

**Idea 10: BottleGraveyard 📱 MOBILE APP**
Wine, whiskey, and craft beer drinkers accumulate bottles over years — gifts from friends, vacation finds, "saving for a special occasion" bottles — and have no way to track what they've opened, what they're cellaring, and most critically, when that bottle's peak drinking window has passed. BottleGraveyard lets you scan a label on opening night, log your tasting notes, set the drinking window (drink within 6 months, age 5 more years, peak in 2028), and build a personal consumption history with ratings over time. When that "special occasion" bottle turns out to be two years past its prime, the app gently tells you so. **Low competition:** Vivino is for wine shopping, Untappd is for beer check-ins — a combined personal cellar manager with aging peak tracking across wine, spirits, and beer in one app doesn't exist. The cork-dork community on Instagram alone would adopt this overnight. **Viral angle:** "The app told me my 'special occasion' whiskey peaked 18 months ago and I should just drink it with friends on a random Tuesday" is deeply relatable content for every person with a dusty bottle collection, and the sheer nerdery of logging tasting notes will create a subculture of competitive reviews.
