## General Ideas (5)

**Idea 1: TenantExit**
When a rental lease ends, tenants have no structured way to prove the property's condition at move-out — resulting in deposit disputes where it's their word vs. the landlord's photos taken a week later. TenantExit is a timestamped move-out documentation tool: room-by-room photo capture with GPS + timestamp metadata, a checklist cross-referenced with the original inventory, and a PDF report emailed instantly to both parties. The legal paper trail that stops "but there was already a scratch there" disputes before they start.

**Idea 2: Speakerfile**
Anyone who speaks at events, podcasts, or conferences knows the pain: every organiser asks for a different format of bio, headshot, topic list, and "speaker rider" — and half the time you're scrambling to find the right file or rewriting your bio from scratch in a Google Doc at 11pm. Speakerfile is a single hosted speaker profile that outputs in every format organisers ask for — short bio, long bio, 100-word version, Twitter-ready, PDF press kit — so you send one link and they pick what they need. Niche, but every conference speaker and podcaster is exactly the same level of disorganised about this.

**Idea 3: RenterRepair**
Tenants in rented properties often don't know which repairs are legally the landlord's responsibility vs. their own, so they either pay for things they shouldn't or don't report things that compound into bigger problems. RenterRepair is a country-specific repair responsibility guide: describe the issue (boiler not heating, window won't close, mould in bathroom), get an instant verdict on who's legally responsible, the correct way to report it in writing, and what to do if the landlord ignores it. Plain-language tenant rights for the specific situation you're actually in right now.

**Idea 4: ProofOfSkill**
Freelancers and career-changers who've built things independently — side projects, open source contributions, self-taught courses — struggle to present that work in a credible way to employers or clients who only trust formal credentials. ProofOfSkill turns informal learning into a structured portfolio: link your GitHub, upload project screenshots, describe what you built and why, and generate a visually clean "work evidence" page that presents practical skills with context. The gap between "I have the skills" and "here's how I can prove it without a degree."

**Idea 5: ContractClock**
Freelancers, consultants, and small agencies perpetually lose track of contract renewal dates, notice periods, and rate review windows — which means either scrambling to renegotiate at the last minute or silently rolling over at the old rate for another year. ContractClock is a contract lifecycle tracker for independents: log each client contract with key dates (start, renewal window, rate review, notice period), get 30-day and 7-day reminders, and keep a changelog of rate history per client. The thing your accountant wishes you were already doing.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: ReviewNudge 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Mobile developers know that app store reviews are dominated by angry users who leave 1-star ratings after a crash, while happy daily users never think to review — leading to a ratings profile that's wildly unrepresentative of actual satisfaction. ReviewNudge is a timing-optimised in-app review prompt SDK: instead of triggering the native review sheet after X launches (the default indie approach), it tracks signals of genuine satisfaction (session streak, feature completion, export/share action) and fires the prompt at the moment of highest positive sentiment — with A/B testing on trigger events built in. Gets you 4-star+ reviews from users who were already going to love you; they just needed to be asked at the right second.

**Idea 7: LandingAudit 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Indie developers spend weeks building a product and 90 minutes on the landing page, then wonder why conversion is 0.8%. The problem is they're too close to it — they know what the product does, so they read their own copy as informative rather than confusing. LandingAudit is a conversion-focused landing page critique tool: paste your URL, and get a structured analysis of clarity (does the hero explain what it does in one sentence?), credibility (is there proof?), friction (how many clicks to trial?), and scannability — with specific rewrite suggestions for each failure point. Not another SEO audit; an actual "why aren't people signing up" diagnosis.

**Idea 8: CronAlert 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Every production system has cron jobs that run silently — nightly data syncs, weekly cleanup scripts, daily email batches — and when they fail, they fail silently: no alert, no log surfaced anywhere obvious, just a report that didn't go out or a database that didn't get pruned. CronAlert is a dead-man's switch monitoring layer for cron jobs: each job pings a unique URL on success; if a ping doesn't arrive in the expected window, you get an immediate alert with the job name, expected schedule, and last successful run time. Zero-config except adding one curl line to your existing cron script — the "you'll be glad you added this" tool for any production codebase with scheduled tasks.

## Mobile App Ideas (2)

**Idea 9: MushroomLog 📱 MOBILE APP**
Foragers who go out regularly to the same spots have no good way to record what they found where and when — most use Notes or voice memos, which are unsearchable and don't carry GPS coordinates — so every season starts from scratch instead of building on last year's knowledge. MushroomLog is a foraging field journal with GPS-tagged finds: log what you found, mark the exact location (shown as vague "zone" not exact pin, for privacy from other foragers), note conditions (weather, substrate, nearby trees), and get seasonal alerts when your logged spots historically start producing. **Low competition:** mushroom ID apps are everywhere, but a forager's personal GPS knowledge base doesn't exist — and the foraging community is large, passionate, secretive about spots, and extremely online. **Viral angle:** "This app shows I've found chanterelles in the same 3m patch every August for 4 years" is the exact post that goes viral in every foraging Facebook group the moment one person shares their heatmap.

**Idea 10: BoardGameChef 📱 MOBILE APP**
Board game groups always end up stuck in analysis paralysis when deciding what to play — someone pulls up BGG, someone wants something quick, someone hates area control, the game voted for takes 4 hours and it's already 9pm. BoardGameChef is a group game picker for people who actually own games: scan or enter your collection, each person at the table taps their preferences (complexity, time, theme, player count), and the app matches against what you own and deals out a ranked shortlist in 30 seconds. **Low competition:** BGG's app is comprehensive but overwhelming; a fast group-consensus tool for "what do we actually play tonight" is completely missing from the market. **Funny/viral angle:** "The app keeps recommending Wingspan and my friends keep vetoing it so now there's a running tally of everyone's most-vetoed game" is exactly the kind of petty social data point that makes a small app feel like a personality test.
