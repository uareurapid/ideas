## General Ideas (5)

**Idea 1: BreakdownChecker**
When a washing machine beeps error code E3 or a boiler starts making a noise, most people's first instinct is to panic and call a repairman — before trying a single fix. BreakdownChecker lets you enter your appliance brand/model and describe the symptom or error code; it returns a ranked diagnostic checklist of likely causes, DIY fix difficulty rating, estimated repair cost, and a "repair vs. replace" recommendation based on appliance age. Save the £120 callout fee with 10 minutes of guided triage before picking up the phone.

**Idea 2: CraftyBOM**
Hobbyist crafters — knitters, leatherworkers, jewelry makers, woodworkers — constantly underestimate materials for a project and end up mid-build with the wrong gauge, wrong quantity, or colour discontinued. CraftyBOM (Bill of Materials) lets you input your pattern or design dimensions and automatically calculates required quantities per material, flags substitutions when a listed item is out of stock, and saves a reorder list with supplier links. The amateur crafter's version of a construction takeoff tool — no more "I'll just guess how much yarn I need."

**Idea 3: ClubDues**
Every sports club, book club, alumni association, and amateur society has a treasurer drowning in a spreadsheet of who paid, who didn't, and who needs chasing for the third time this month. ClubDues is a lightweight dues collection tool for voluntary associations: add members, set renewal dates, send automated payment reminders via WhatsApp or email, track paid/outstanding status, and generate a one-click treasurer's report at AGM time. Not a full CRM — just the "who owes what and when" layer every club treasurer does manually in Excel right now.

**Idea 4: DeliveryAlert**
Online shoppers in apartment buildings miss deliveries constantly because "arriving Tuesday between 8am and 8pm" is useless for planning. DeliveryAlert aggregates all your active shipments (Amazon, DHL, FedEx, CTT, UPS) into one feed, monitors driver location data where available, and fires a push notification 20–40 minutes before your specific package is next in the route — so you're actually home or by the door. The missing "get to the intercom before the attempted delivery slip" tool for city dwellers.

**Idea 5: RehearsalKit**
Cover bands, wedding bands, and gigging musicians manage their setlist, song ownership per member, and rehearsal schedule across six WhatsApp messages, a sticky note, and someone's memory. RehearsalKit is a band management micro-tool: build your song library, mark which members know each track, schedule rehearsals with attendance tracking, and share the running order the morning of a gig. Aimed squarely at the 3–6 person amateur-to-semi-pro gigging band — not touring management software, just the coordination layer that doesn't currently exist.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: OffboardingScripts 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
When a SaaS customer hits the cancel button, most indie apps just delete the account or let it go silently — missing the single highest-leverage moment to recover churn or capture insight. OffboardingScripts is a configurable cancellation workflow builder: trigger an exit survey, offer a plan pause or downgrade, queue a personal founder email at day 3, schedule a data export reminder, and fire a 30-day win-back sequence — all from a single webhook. Setup takes an afternoon; it consistently recovers 5–15% of churning users that would have just disappeared without a word.

**Idea 7: ReleaseNoteWriter 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Developers ship code constantly but writing human-readable release notes is a tedious context-switch — most indie devs either skip it or dump raw commit hashes. ReleaseNoteWriter connects to your GitHub repo, clusters merged PRs and commits by type (feature / fix / security / breaking change), generates polished release notes in your product's existing tone, and pushes them to GitHub Releases, your hosted changelog page, or your email list with one click. The gap between `git log` and something your users actually want to read — closed in under 5 minutes per release.

**Idea 8: PrivacyPolicyKeeper 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Indie SaaS founders update their apps constantly — new OAuth integrations, new analytics tools, new data fields — but almost never update their privacy policy to reflect the change, quietly accumulating GDPR/CCPA liability they don't know about. PrivacyPolicyKeeper scans your landing page, docs, and declared third-party integrations on a weekly basis, compares against your current published privacy policy, and flags likely discrepancies: "you added Google Analytics last month but your policy says you use no third-party analytics." Not a legal service — a legal *hygiene* alarm for solo founders with no counsel and too many deployments.

## Mobile App Ideas (2)

**Idea 9: PlantHospital 📱 MOBILE APP**
Millions of people are actively killing their houseplants but don't know why — generic plant ID apps tell you what you have, not what's wrong with it. PlantHospital is a sick-plant diagnostic triage tool: describe symptoms (yellowing, drooping, brown tips, spots), add a photo, and it walks through a structured diagnostic tree to identify the likely cause — overwatering, root rot, pest, low humidity, wrong light — then generates a step-by-step treatment protocol and a "recovery log" to track whether the intervention worked over the following 2 weeks. **Low competition:** the App Store is full of plant ID apps; sick-plant *triage* with a recovery tracker is almost nonexistent. **Viral angle:** "Is my plant dying or just being dramatic?" is deeply relatable content — every plant parent has a murder story to tell.

**Idea 10: TramBingo 📱 MOBILE APP**
Urban commuters on metros, trams, and buses experience the exact same recurring cast of characters every day — the manspreader, the loud-phone-call person, the someone-eating-something-smelly, the unexplained delay — but there's no way to channel that shared suffering into something fun. TramBingo generates daily bingo cards filled with commuter archetypes and transit situations; tap a square when you spot it in the wild, complete a row for a streak, and optionally compete with friends who take the same line. **Low competition:** zero apps exist in this exact problem space because it sounds silly — which is precisely why it's wide open. **Viral angle:** "Got a bingo by stop 4, the tram ate someone's entire lunch" is instant shareable content; the app practically markets itself through screenshots.
