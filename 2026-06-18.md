## General Ideas (5)

**Idea 1: AirClaim**
Airlines bank on passengers abandoning compensation claims because the process is deliberately confusing — EU261, UK CAA, DOT regulations all have different rules, deadlines, and evidence requirements, and the airline's first response is almost always a rejection template hoping you'll give up. AirClaim takes your flight number, disruption details, and reason given by the airline, instantly determines eligibility under the applicable regulation, generates a legally-referenced claim letter with the exact compensation amount you're owed, and tracks the claim through resolution with escalation prompts when the airline goes silent. The €250-600 per passenger that airlines count on you never claiming, automated into a 5-minute process.

**Idea 2: BorrowLog**
Everyone has items circulating among friends and neighbors they've lost track of — the pressure washer went to someone, the folding chairs left with the block party organizer, the camping tent is "somewhere in the friend group" — but asking for stuff back feels awkward and accusatory. BorrowLog is a dead-simple lending memory: snap a photo of the item, tag the recipient, set a return window, and get an automated "your item is due back" nudge that removes the social friction from the conversation. Not an inventory app — a social memory layer that turns "I'm too embarrassed to ask if they still have my drill" into a neutral notification from an app.

**Idea 3: ShiftParent**
Co-parents across two households manage school events, doctor appointments, sports schedules, and parent-teacher conferences through fragmented texts and shared calendars that can't handle alternating-week patterns, holiday splits, or last-minute swap requests — turning every scheduling conversation into a negotiation neither parent has emotional bandwidth for. ShiftParent is a co-parenting coordination layer: set your custody pattern once (alternating weeks, 2-2-3, custom), auto-assign school events to whoever has the child that day, log medical appointments with notes both parents can access, and handle swap requests through a structured flow that keeps the negotiation functional rather than personal. For the millions of separated parents who get along fine but are exhausted by the admin overhead.

**Idea 4: ClaimSmart**
When your home floods, catches fire, or gets burgled, you're thrown into an insurance claims process while emotionally shattered — you don't know how to document damage properly, what your policy actually covers, or whether the adjuster's settlement offer is fair, and you're negotiating against professionals who do this daily while you're doing it for the first time on zero sleep. ClaimSmart is a step-by-step disaster claims guide: room-by-room damage documentation with photo guidance (what to capture, what angles, what details adjusters look for), policy coverage analysis in plain English, comparable repair cost estimates to benchmark against the adjuster's offer, and a timeline tracker so nothing expires during a process that can drag on for months. The insurance claims process is deliberately asymmetrical — this levels it.

**Idea 5: TipTransparency**
Tipping culture has spiraled into absurdity — point-of-sale tablets now prompt for 25% tips at self-serve kiosks, coffee counters, and online pickup orders where zero human service occurred — but there's no shared reference for what's actually expected vs. what's just aggressive software defaults, and customers feel guilt-tripped into overpaying or awkward about hitting "no tip" in front of the cashier. TipTransparency is a crowd-sourced guide to real tipping norms by establishment type, country, and context: users report what the POS prompted, what's actually customary, whether staff receive the digital tip, and whether any service was involved. The "should I actually tip on this screen or is the software just asking everyone" reference that finally exists.

## Developer Tools (3)

**Idea 6: CompetitorRadar 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Indie founders know they should track competitors but the process is pure chaos — bookmarking competitor sites, checking occasionally, noticing a new pricing tier three months after it launched, and having no structured way to compare positioning shifts over time. CompetitorRadar monitors competitor homepages, pricing pages, changelogs, and feature announcements for diffs — new features, pricing changes, copy rewrites, new integrations — and sends a weekly digest with screenshots of exactly what changed, ranked by significance. The "wait, when did they add that?" problem solved with automated change detection instead of manual browser-tab stalking.

**Idea 7: PackageHealth 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Every npm/pip/cargo dependency you add is a bet on that package's future — will the maintainer burn out? Are there unaddressed issues piling up? Is the project effectively abandoned despite recent patch releases? PackageHealth scores your dependency tree: analyzes release cadence, maintainer activity and bus factor, issue response time, open vulnerability count, and community health signals, then flags specific "at risk" packages before they become your problem. Like a credit score for your node_modules — run it before adding a dependency, or monthly as a maintenance health check that tells you which package is one burned-out maintainer away from becoming your tech debt.

**Idea 8: FeedbackFilter 🛠️ DEV TOOL**
Indie developers with users get feedback from everywhere — App Store reviews, Twitter mentions, Reddit threads, support emails, Discord messages — and have no way to aggregate, deduplicate, and prioritize the chaos into an actual development roadmap. FeedbackFilter connects to your review platforms, social mentions, and support channels, clusters similar feedback using semantic matching, counts mentions per feature request, and surfaces actionable signals: "14 people asked for dark mode this month" and "3 power users threatening to churn over missing CSV export." Converts the firehose of scattered user feedback into a prioritized backlog without requiring a full-time product manager you can't afford.

## Mobile App Ideas (2)

**Idea 9: BinNight 📱 MOBILE APP**
Entire streets play "copy the neighbor" at 10pm on bin night because nobody can remember whether it's recycling, general waste, or garden waste this week — and bank holidays make the schedule shift in ways nobody tracks until the truck doesn't come. BinNight fixes this: enter your postcode once, get your personalized collection calendar with bin-type indicators per week, automatic bank holiday adjustments, and a notification the evening before with exactly which bin(s) go out tomorrow. **Low competition:** a handful of UK councils have their own apps, but a universal postcode-based "which bin tonight" layer that works across regions doesn't exist — and the gap between "my council has a PDF somewhere" and "my phone told me it's recycling tonight" is enormous. **Viral angle:** the optional "street compliance" feature showing how many houses on your block have already put bins out creates exactly the kind of low-stakes neighborhood rivalry that group chats are built on, and the "it's recycling week and my neighbor put out general waste — the chaos has begun" content is infinitely shareable.

**Idea 10: WeatherRegret 📱 MOBILE APP**
Everyone has stood outside in completely wrong clothing for the actual weather — convinced it would be warm, wearing a jumper in 28°C heat, or forgetting that "sunny" at 9am means "freezing wind" by 3pm — and weather apps give you data, not decisions. WeatherRegret flips the model: you log what you wore and rate your regret level against what actually happened, and over time the app learns YOUR personal comfort thresholds — maybe you run cold at 18°C while the forecast says "mild," or you always overdress on overcast days that clear up. The recommendation becomes "based on 47 times you regretted wearing a jacket when it hit 22°C, today looks like a t-shirt day for you specifically." **Low competition:** every weather app tells you the temperature; none learn from your documented history of being wrong about it. **Viral angle:** "My app just told me 'you have dressed for the weather you want, not the weather you have, 63 times this year' and I have never been so personally attacked by a weather forecast" — the quantified self-meets-weather-regret angle is exactly the kind of content that screenshots its way across every social platform.
